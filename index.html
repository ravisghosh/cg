The Problem

The way we house ourselves is broken.
And we've accepted it as normal.

Why housing is broken

Affordability — the treadmill that never stops

In Bengaluru, the average apartment costs 8–12× the average annual household income. Most families spend 40–50% of their take-home pay on rent or EMIs — leaving little for savings, health, education, or simply living well.

The problem isn't a shortage of construction. It's that housing has been turned into a financial asset — something to be bought, sold, and speculated on — rather than what it fundamentally is: a place to live.

Isolation — the hidden epidemic

Move into a new apartment complex and count how many neighbours you know by name after one year. For most people, the answer is two or three — if that. Modern housing maximises privacy and minimises connection. Every unit is sealed off. Every family cooks alone, drives alone, worries alone.

Research is unambiguous: loneliness is as damaging to health as smoking 15 cigarettes a day. It accelerates cognitive decline, raises blood pressure, and shortens lives. We've designed loneliness into our homes.

Inflexibility — one home for a changing life

A young professional needs a compact, connected studio. A growing family needs bedrooms and outdoor space. An empty nester wants ease and community. A retiree needs accessibility and care proximity. Yet most people buy one home and try to make it work forever — or go through the exhausting, expensive process of selling and buying again at each transition.

The hidden costs of conventional housing

The price of your flat is just the beginning.

Financial costs

  • Developer premium: 20–35% of your purchase price is profit and marketing
  • Home loan interest: over 20 years, you pay roughly double the principal
  • Maintenance fees that keep rising with no accountability
  • Brokerage (1–2%) every time you buy or sell
  • Individual service costs — each negotiated weakly, alone

Social & environmental costs

  • No built-in community; you must build it yourself, if at all
  • No shared childcare, eldercare, or support systems
  • No fallback when life gets hard
  • Individual appliances and systems, each underutilised
  • No pooled solar, water harvesting, or waste management

The gap between a house and a home

A house is coordinates on a map. A home is where you belong.

People who've lived in genuine communities — co-housing in Denmark, cooperative housing in Uruguay, intentional communities across the world — consistently describe the same thing: a sense of being held. Knowing that if you fall ill, someone will notice. That your children have adults who know their names. That old age doesn't have to mean waiting alone.

That's not nostalgia. It's a design choice. Communities like this exist. They function. They outlast their founders. They produce healthier, happier, longer-lived residents. We can build that here.

Our Vision

Not a building.
A way of living.

Community over commodity.

"Housing is not a product to be consumed. It is a commons to be built together."

In a cooperative, there are no developers extracting profit. No landlords raising rent at will. No speculative flipping. Instead, members pool resources, make decisions together, and collectively own something that serves all of them — not a distant shareholder.

This isn't idealism. It's a tested, proven model. Housing cooperatives house over 1.2 billion people worldwide across Europe, Latin America, North America and beyond. The Danish co-housing movement has been thriving for over 50 years. Uruguay's FUCVAM cooperative has built over 25,000 homes for working families.

We're not inventing something new. We're bringing something time-tested to Bengaluru.

The Blue Zones connection.

Where people live longest — and why

In the late 2000s, researcher Dan Buettner identified five regions where people routinely live past 100 in good health — Okinawa, Sardinia, Nicoya, Ikaria, and Loma Linda. The longest-lived people in the world don't have exceptional genes or secret diets. They share specific lifestyle patterns — and the most powerful is belonging.

🌐 Strong social networks — people who check in on each other daily
🎯 Shared purpose — contributing to something beyond oneself
🚶 Natural movement — activity built into daily life, not scheduled
🧘 Stress reduction rituals — built into community rhythms
🍽️ Communal eating — meals as social events, not solitary refuelling
🏘️ Regular community gathering — shared practices and belonging

Our cooperative is designed around every one of these principles — not as a wellness programme layered on top, but as the architecture of how we live.

Our long-term vision is a network of cooperative communities — across the city, and eventually across India — sharing a legal framework, a governance playbook, and a culture of mutual aid. Each community self-governing. Each connected to the larger whole.

Our Model

How it actually works —
ownership, community, and decisions.

What is cooperative tenure?

In a conventional purchase, you buy a flat. In our cooperative, you become a member. You pay a membership share — a capital contribution that gives you the right to occupy your home. This is not rent. But it is also not speculative ownership.

What you own

Your membership share, the right to your home for life, a proportional stake in collective assets, and a vote in every major decision.

What you don't own

The land (held collectively), the ability to sell at market price, or to sublet freely. This is by design — it's what keeps housing affordable.

Why this structure?

The moment homes become speculative assets, affordability is destroyed. Collective land ownership ensures the community remains affordable for the next generation too.

How does the money work?

Indicative model — to be finalised with founding members and legal/financial advisors.

Entry

Members contribute a capital share reflecting home size and community investment — significantly below equivalent market cost.

Monthly

A carrying cost covering maintenance, shared services, and sinking fund — well below market rent or EMI equivalents.

Exit

Your capital share is returned, adjusted for inflation — not market appreciation. No windfall, but no loss either.

One community, your whole life.

The average Indian family moves home 3–4 times over a lifetime. Each move costs lakhs in fees, months of stress, and the loss of community you'd built. In our model, you move within the community.

What "shared" actually looks like.

This isn't about giving up your privacy. It's about gaining abundance.

Community kitchen

Optional daily meals at ₹50–80 per meal. Saves 2–3 hours daily. A rotating team of residents cooks alongside paid staff. You can always cook in your own kitchen — but you don't have to.

Group health insurance

100+ adults have enormous negotiating power. Our group policy covers all members, managed by a community health committee that holds the insurer accountable. No more claim denials going uncontested.

Community solar

Rooftop solar serving the entire community reduces per-unit energy costs dramatically. Surplus fed to the grid. Battery storage for resilience during outages.

Water harvesting

Rainwater harvesting, greywater recycling, and bore well management at community scale. Bengaluru's water challenges are far easier to address collectively than household by household.

Tool library

One community drill, one pressure washer, one sewing machine. Expensive, rarely-used equipment owned collectively and borrowed freely. Thousands saved per household annually.

The safety net

An emergency fund for members in crisis. Community labour exchange. Coordinated support during illness or bereavement. In conventional housing, you face these moments alone.

You run this place.

We are a cooperative, not a company. There are no shareholders to please, no board that overrides residents. Power lives with the members.

The General Assembly

All members. One vote each. Meets monthly. Sets priorities, approves major budgets, resolves disputes, and amends community agreements.

Working Committees

Residents self-organise into committees — maintenance, food, community life, finance, green infrastructure. Each holds operational authority in its domain.

The Stewardship Council

A small elected body (5–7 members) handling day-to-day coordination and external matters. Elected annually. Term-limited. Reports to the Assembly.

Conflict Resolution

Disputes go first to mediation, facilitated by trained community members. If unresolved, to the Assembly. We avoid litigation by design.

Decision tiers

Routine: Committee authority  ·  Significant operational: Council authority  ·  Major (budget, policy, membership): General Assembly  ·  Constitutional changes: Supermajority of all members

Frequently asked
Is this legal in India?+
Yes. Housing cooperatives are a recognised legal structure under the Cooperative Societies Acts of various states, including Karnataka. We are working with legal advisors to determine the optimal structure — Cooperative Society, Group Housing Cooperative, or a hybrid.
Is this like a housing society or RWA?+
Not quite. In a conventional housing society, you own your flat individually and join the society. In a cooperative, the cooperative owns the property collectively and you own a membership share. This is what enables lifecycle flexibility, anti-speculation pricing, and genuine democratic governance.
Do I lose money if housing prices go up?+
Your capital contribution appreciates with inflation, not market speculation. You don't capture market upside — but you also don't face market downside, and you save significantly every year through shared costs. The model is optimised for quality of life, not investment return.
What if I want to leave?+
You give notice per the community agreement. Your capital share is returned. Your unit is offered to the next member on the housing register. There is no penalty for leaving — though we hope you'll stay.
Can I customise my home?+
Within agreed parameters, yes. Structural changes require community approval. Interior decisions are yours.
What about inheritance?+
Membership shares can be transferred to family members who wish to join the community, subject to the community membership process. Details will be developed during the founding phase.
I rent currently — can I join?+
Yes. The entry model includes options for members who cannot make a full capital contribution upfront. Rent-to-membership pathways are part of the design.

Global examples

Spain

La Borda, Barcelona

Built in 2018 on public land leased for 75 years. Combines private dwellings with extensive common spaces — kitchen, laundry, workshop. Governed entirely by residents. A landmark project for European cooperative housing.

Uruguay

FUCVAM

The world's most successful model of worker-built cooperative housing. Since 1970, FUCVAM affiliates have built over 25,000 homes using mutual aid — members contribute their own labour in exchange for lower costs. Replicated across Latin America.

Denmark

Danish Bofællesskaber

Over 700 co-housing communities, typically 15–35 private dwellings around shared spaces. Residents cook together several times a week. Studies consistently show higher wellbeing and lower loneliness than conventional housing.

India

Auroville, Tamil Nadu

Founded 1968. Over 3,500 residents of 60 nationalities. Demonstrates that large-scale community living is possible in the Indian context — with remarkable social and environmental outcomes.

UK

LILAC, Leeds

Low Impact Living Affordable Community — a Mutual Home Ownership Society where members pay a percentage of income rather than a fixed mortgage, making entry genuinely accessible regardless of savings.

India · Legal

Cooperative Housing in India

Housing cooperatives are recognised under the Cooperative Societies Acts of various states, including Karnataka. NCHI (National Cooperative Housing Federation of India) provides national regulatory context.

Honesty over hype

Community over commodity
